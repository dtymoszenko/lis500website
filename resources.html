Understanding Intersectionality

Intersectionality is a way of understanding and analyzing the complexity in the world, in people, and in human experiences. The events and conditions of social and political life and the self can seldom be understood as shaped by one factor. They are generally shaped by many factors in diverse and mutually influencing ways. When it comes to social inequality, people's lives and the organization of power in a given society are better understood as being shaped not by a single axis of social division, be it race or gender or class, but my many axes that work together and influence each other. Intersectionality as an analytic tool gives people better access to the complexity of the world and of themselves.


-Patricia Hill Collins & Sirma Bilge (2016)


In other words, Intersectionality is a framework for understanding the complexities of human identities and how those complexities interact to influence social inequality, people's lives, and the organization of power.


Understanding Implicit Bias

Implicit bias impacts how we perceive other people in ways we don’t consciously realize, often reinforcing harmful stereotypes and rhetoric. Implicit association tests (IAT”s) were developed in the late 1990’s and have been used ever since to increase people's awareness of the unconscious impact their implicit biases have on their perceptions of different groups of people.

Understanding How Implicit Bias and Intersectionality Interact

We all have implicit biases whether we realize it or not. But how do these implicit biases come about? And what do they tell us about ourselves and society as a whole? Implicit biases are a result of the social, economic, and political climates we were raised in. In addition to our environments, our implicit biases are also influenced by race, gender, ethnicity, culture, and other factors. Many of these factors were mentioned in Patricia and Sirma’s definition of intersectionality. The theory of intersectionality helps us to make sense of our implicit biases. But our implicit biases are not the direct result of factors like our race, gender, or economic status. They come from the culmination of all of the different aspects of our identities and the interactions between these aspects and the world around us. Ultimately, understanding where our implicit biases come from is the first step in expanding our awareness of our biases and addressing them. Intersectionality is an analytical tool that helps us reach this awareness by highlighting the ways in which people's overlapping identities intersect to create unique instances of social oppression or advantage.

Our Findings

In our experience taking the Implicit Bias tests, we both reached eye-opening revelations about how our implicit biases manifest themselves. Cy was tested on his biases regarding disabled people. David took the tests for age and weight. In Cy’s experience, he was stunned to discover that he did have a bias against disabled people according to the American Bar Association’s Implicit Bias test. David discovered that he had a positive preference toward people who were younger and on the thinner side.


How Can Our Understanding of Intersectionality and Implicit Biases Help Us To Make Sense Of Our Findings?

A good place to start is to decipher how the “Many axes that work together and influence each other” (Hill Collins, Bilge 2016) interacted to impact our Implicit biases. In Cy’s case, his upbringing in an able-bodied household interacting with his experience as an able-bodied person may have impacted his biases and apparent lack of empathy for disabled people. Similarly, David’s experience growing up in a household that was made up of generally thinner people and his own aptitude for physical fitness may have interacted to impact his weight implicit bias. His living in a college town surrounded by younger people and his own young age may have interacted to impact his implicit bias towards younger people. It is important to note that our implicit biases are not solely the result of two factors of our identities interacting. In accordance with the theory of intersectionality, our implicit biases are the result of a multitude of different aspects of our identities, societal, and environmental factors. The examples listed above are some of the most obvious factors. In reality the endeavor of understanding the roots of our implicit biases is an incredibly complex and multidimensional challenge. Regardless, our use of the theory of intersectionality gives us a better understanding of this challenge and provides a framework for better understanding our own identities and our relationship with the world around us.
